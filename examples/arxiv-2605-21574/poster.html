Little red dots are not just a JWST-era high-z curiosity

DESI finds 27 z = 0.2-0.9 counterparts with the same spectral fingerprint, turning LRDs into a population we can study nearby and across cosmic time.

(LRDs)2: The Low-ReDshift Little Red Dots Survey. II. DESI DR1 Sample Xiaojing Lin et al. | 2026 | arXiv:2605.21574

Background

Little red dots may mark a strange, common mode of black-hole growth. If they exist only in JWST high-z data, they stay exotic; if they also exist nearby, they become testable.

Knowledge gap: before this work, we did not know whether the JWST LRD phenomenon survives into the low-z universe in a statistically convincing, spectroscopically selected sample.

What this paper is selling

This paper builds a DESI DR1 sample of 27 LRDs at z < 1 and argues they are the same family as the high-z JWST objects, not just red oddball AGN. That turns low-z LRDs into nearby laboratories and hints that cool, large envelopes may be more common at late times.

Key results to remember

  • The 27 DESI objects reproduce the full LRD signature package and overlap the high-z JWST population in luminosity and continuum shape, favoring a shared phenomenon rather than a separate low-z class.
  • Broad Balmer lines miss normal type-1 AGN scaling, and several sources peak at > 1 micron, pointing to unusual BH-mass calibrations and especially cool, large envelopes.
